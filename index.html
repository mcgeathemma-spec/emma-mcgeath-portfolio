About me

I am a Senior Instructional Systems Designer with a systematic approach to training development. I carry projects through every phase, from needs assessment through evaluation, and care just as much about the thinking behind a course as the experience of taking it.

My specialty is high-fidelity, highly interactive courseware: simulations, branching scenarios, gamified skill progressions, and complex interactive multimedia that mirrors the demands of real-world environments. I bring human factors principles into my design process to ensure training is not only engaging but genuinely effective.

My work is grounded in a strong academic foundation in psychology and research. I have contributed to human factors research labs and bring that same rigor to how I approach instructional problems, treating design as a discipline informed by evidence, not intuition.

Instructional Systems Design Models

My process draws from established models and adapts based on what the project actually needs. The models that I use the most are listed below, though my approach is not limited to them.

  • Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, and Evaluation (ADDIE)
  • Successive Approximation Model (SAM)
  • Dick, Carey, and Carey systematic design model
Analysis Methodologies

When I have the opportunity to do the analysis work, I make the most of it. It informs everything that comes after.

  • Training needs analysis and performance gap analysis
  • Learner, task, and context analysis
  • Cognitive Task Analysis
  • Risk and consequence analysis
Modalities

I work across a range of delivery modalities and am comfortable moving between them. What is listed below represents my most common experience.

  • eLearning (asynchronous self-paced)
  • Instructor-led training (ILT)
  • Interactive courseware (ICW/IMI)
  • Synchronous virtual instruction
  • Blended learning
  • Scenario-based and simulation training
  • Gamified learning experiences
Instructional Deliverables

The deliverables I produce are shaped by the project. The list below captures common outputs but every engagement looks a little different.

  • Learning objectives and course sequencing documents
  • Interaction design specifications and storyboards
  • Style guides, evaluation plans, and assessments
Cognitive and Learning Design Frameworks

My practice is informed by learning science research and I work to stay current as the field evolves. The frameworks below are the ones I draw on most consistently, though they represent a portion of a broader foundation.

  • Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller)
  • Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (Mayer)
  • Bloom's Taxonomy, Revised
  • Gagné's Nine Events of Instruction
  • Keller's Attention, Relevance, Confidence, and Satisfaction (ARCS) Motivation Model
  • Learning Experience Design
  • Adult learning theory
